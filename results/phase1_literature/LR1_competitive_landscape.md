## Executive Summary

**No one has published a Zoonomia-scale chromosomal rearrangement atlas for Coleoptera.** However, Bracewell et al. (2024) established "Stevens elements" — ancestral linkage groups for beetles — using 12 genomes. Our project with 400+ genomes represents a ~35× scale-up and a fundamentally different scope (whole-genome synteny atlas with ancestral karyotype reconstruction, not just sex chromosome evolution). The Lepidoptera field is ahead of us with Wright et al. (2024) analyzing 210 genomes. We need to position clearly against both.

**Scooping risk: LOW-MEDIUM.** No competing beetle synteny atlas is visible, but the Bracewell group could scale up. Speed matters.

## 1. Zoonomia-Style Projects (Multi-Genome Comparative Atlases)

### 1.1 Zoonomia Consortium (Mammals) — The Gold Standard

- **Christmas et al. (2023)** *Science*. 240 mammal genomes. Evolutionary constraint, conservation.
  - DOI: 10.1126/science.abn3943 | Cited: 248
- **Genereux et al. (2020)** *Nature*. Comparative genomics multitool. 131 mammals.
  - DOI: 10.1038/s41586-020-2876-6 | Cited: 420
- **Foley et al. (2023)** *Science*. Genomic timescale for placental mammals. 241 genomes.
  - DOI: 10.1126/science.abl8189 | Cited: 145
- **Key lesson:** Zoonomia succeeded because it combined massive taxon sampling with clear biological insights (constraint, adaptation, conservation). Our beetle atlas should follow this formula.

### 1.2 Damas et al. (2022) — Ancestral Mammalian Karyotype

- **Damas et al. (2022)** *PNAS*. Ancestral karyotypes at 16 nodes using 34 genome assemblies. 19 ancestral autosome pairs. Classified rearrangement types branch-by-branch.
  - DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2209139119 | Cited: 93
- **Direct methodological template for our project.** Their approach (reconstruct ancestral karyotypes → classify rearrangements → map to phylogeny) is exactly what we're planning for beetles. We should cite this as a model and compare our rates to theirs.

### 1.3 Lepidoptera — Wright et al. (2024) — Our Closest Competitor

- **Wright et al. (2024)** *Nature Ecology & Evolution*. 210 chromosome-level lepidopteran genomes. Identified 32 ancestral linkage groups ("Merian elements"). Found remarkable stability over 250 My, with 8 lineages showing extensive reorganization.
  - DOI: 10.1038/s41559-024-02329-4 | Cited: rapidly accumulating
- **This is the paper we need to beat or match in scope.** Published in Nature Eco Evo, which is our target-tier journal. Their key advance: reference-free, phylogenetically aware approach to infer ancestral linkage groups. Fusions biased toward shorter autosomes and Z chromosome.
- **Our unique angle vs. Lepidoptera:** Beetles have ~10× more species, more karyotypic diversity, and we can leverage the existing karyotype database (Blackmon lab) for validation. Beetle karyotypes are more variable than Lepidoptera (2n = 4 to 2n = 70+), making the atlas more interesting.

### 1.4 Drosophila — Kim et al. (2021)

- **Kim et al. (2021)** *eLife*. 101 drosophilid genomes, high contiguity.
  - DOI: 10.7554/elife.66405 | Cited: 215
- **Muller elements** are the original "ancestral linkage groups" concept. Stevens elements in beetles are directly analogous.

## 2. Beetle-Specific Chromosome Evolution Studies

### 2.1 Bracewell et al. (2024) — Stevens Elements ⚠️ KEY COMPETITOR

- **Bracewell, Tran, Chatla & Bachtrog (2024)** *PLOS Genetics*. "Sex and neo-sex chromosome evolution in beetles."
  - DOI: 10.1371/journal.pgen.1011477 | Cited: 13
  - Used 12 beetle genomes (including new T. confusum assembly)
  - **Defined 9 "Stevens elements"** — ancestral beetle linkage groups
  - Focused on sex chromosome evolution and neo-sex chromosomes
  - Named after Nettie Stevens (T. molitor XY discovery)

- **Implications for us:**
  1. **Stevens elements are already defined.** We don't get to discover them. BUT: they used only 12 genomes. We have 400+.
  2. **Our value-add:** (a) Fine-scale rearrangement mapping across 400+ species, (b) ancestral karyotype reconstruction at 25+ internal nodes (they did minimal ancestral reconstruction), (c) rearrangement rate variation across the beetle tree, (d) hotspot identification, (e) comparison to the published karyotype literature (Blackmon lab database).
  3. **We should explicitly build on Stevens elements** — validate them with our much larger dataset and extend the framework.

### 2.2 Copeland et al. (2024) — Dendroctonus (TAMU / Blackmon Lab)

- **Copeland, Landa, Owoyemi, Jonika, Alfieri, Johnston, Sylvester, Kyre, Hoover, Hjelmen, Rieske, Blackmon & Casola (2024)** *Royal Society Open Science*. Southern pine beetle genome.
  - DOI: 10.1098/rsos.240755 | Cited: 1
  - **Confirmed conservation of Stevens elements in Dendroctonus**
  - Synteny analysis validated the bona fide SPB X chromosome
  - **Heath is a co-author** — establishes our lab's track record with beetle chromosome evolution

### 2.3 Blackmon Lab — Foundational Karyotype Work

- **Blackmon & Demuth (2014)** *Genetics*. 4,724 beetle karyotypes. Y chromosome gain/loss rates. "Fragile Y hypothesis."
  - DOI: 10.1534/genetics.114.164269 | Cited: 68
- **Blackmon, Ross & Bachtrog (2016)** *J. Heredity*. 13,000+ insect karyotypes database. Sex determination, sex chromosomes, karyotype evolution.
  - DOI: 10.1093/jhered/esw047 | Cited: 224
- **Ruckman, Jonika, Casola & Blackmon (2020)** *PLOS Genetics*. 4,393 insect species. Fission/fusion/polyploidy rates. Holocentric vs monocentric.
  - DOI: 10.1371/journal.pgen.1009076 | Cited: 42
- **Our lab has the largest beetle karyotype database in the world.** This is a unique validation resource no other group can match.

### 2.4 Toups & Viçoso (2023) — Ancient Insect X Chromosome

- **Toups & Viçoso (2023)** *Evolution*. Insect X chromosome likely predates class Insecta (>450 My).
  - DOI: 10.1093/evolut/qpad169 | Cited: 33
- Relevant context: the ancestral X is conserved across insects; Stevens element that corresponds to the X should be traceable.

### 2.5 Other Beetle Genome Studies

- **Keeling et al. (2021)** *Mol Ecol Resources*. Chromosome-level Dendroctonus ponderosae. Identified Stevens elements correspondence.
  - DOI: 10.1111/1755-0998.13528 | Cited: 35
- **Euwallacea ambrosia beetles (2024)** Chromosome structural rearrangements. Haplodiploid complication.
  - PMID: 39431789
- **Adalia bipunctata (2024)** Satellitome analysis. Chromosomal fusions between species.
  - DOI: 10.3390/ijms25179214

## 6. Our Unique Contribution (Positioning Statement)

**What makes our project novel:**

1. **Scale:** ~400 beetle genomes vs. 12 in Bracewell et al. The largest chromosomal rearrangement atlas for any insect order.
2. **Ancestral reconstruction:** Full karyotype reconstruction at 25+ internal nodes across the beetle phylogeny (Bracewell didn't do this).
3. **Rearrangement rate variation:** Branch-specific rates, hotspot identification, association with speciation — these are not addressed by existing studies.
4. **Validation against karyotype data:** The Blackmon lab karyotype database (4,700+ beetle species) provides an independent validation resource no other group has.
5. **Resource:** Public release of synteny blocks, rearrangement calls, ancestral karyotypes — a community resource comparable to Zoonomia.
6. **Building on Stevens elements:** We validate and extend the 9 Stevens elements across a much broader taxonomic sample, potentially refining the ancestral beetle karyotype.

**Framing:** "Bracewell et al. (2024) identified 9 ancestral linkage groups (Stevens elements) using 12 beetle genomes. Here, we leverage >400 chromosome-level genomes spanning 61 families to construct the first comprehensive chromosomal rearrangement atlas for Coleoptera, the most species-rich eukaryotic order."

## 7. Recommended Journal Strategy (Revised)

Given the competitive landscape:

1. **Primary target: Nature Ecology & Evolution** — Wright et al. (Lepidoptera) published here; beetle equivalent should be appropriate. Strong comparative genomics audience.
2. **Alternative 1: Molecular Biology and Evolution** — Core evolutionary genomics audience. Faster review.
3. **Alternative 2: Current Biology** — If we have a particularly striking finding (e.g., unexpected rearrangement burst).
4. **Alternative 3: Genome Research** — Resource emphasis.
